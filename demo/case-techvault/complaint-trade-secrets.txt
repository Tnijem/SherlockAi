Plaintiff TechVault Solutions, Inc. ("TechVault"), by and through its attorneys, Brennan, Ortiz & Calloway LLP, alleges as follows:

INTRODUCTION

1. This case involves the brazen theft of proprietary encryption technology by a former senior engineer who left TechVault to join a direct competitor, taking with him TechVault's most closely guarded trade secrets — the source code, architecture documents, and algorithmic specifications for TechVault's "VaultLock" zero-knowledge encryption platform.

PARTIES

2. Plaintiff TechVault Solutions, Inc. is a Delaware corporation with its principal place of business at 2900 Stevens Creek Boulevard, San Jose, California 95128. TechVault develops enterprise data security and encryption solutions, with annual revenue of approximately $85 million.

3. Defendant NexGen Data Systems, LLC ("NexGen") is a Delaware limited liability company with its principal place of business at 1600 Amphitheatre Parkway, Mountain View, California 94043. NexGen is a direct competitor of TechVault in the enterprise encryption market.

4. Defendant Kevin P. Zhang ("Zhang") is a resident of Santa Clara County, California. Zhang was employed by TechVault as a Senior Cryptographic Engineer from March 2022 through December 31, 2025. He joined NexGen as Vice President of Engineering on January 15, 2026.

FACTUAL ALLEGATIONS

The VaultLock Platform

5. TechVault's VaultLock platform is a zero-knowledge encryption system that allows enterprise customers to encrypt sensitive data such that even TechVault cannot access it. VaultLock's competitive advantage lies in its proprietary key derivation algorithm ("KDA-7"), which achieves industry-leading performance while maintaining FIPS 140-3 compliance.

6. TechVault has invested over $12 million and approximately 47,000 engineering hours over four years developing the VaultLock platform and KDA-7 algorithm.

7. The VaultLock source code, architectural designs, algorithmic specifications, customer integration patterns, and performance benchmarks constitute trade secrets under both the Defend Trade Secrets Act, 18 U.S.C. § 1836, and the California Uniform Trade Secrets Act, Cal. Civ. Code § 3426.

8. TechVault maintains reasonable measures to protect these trade secrets, including:
   (a) Access controls limiting source code access to authorized engineers;
   (b) Two-factor authentication and VPN requirements for all code repositories;
   (c) Mandatory non-disclosure and invention assignment agreements for all employees;
   (d) Non-competition and non-solicitation agreements (to the extent enforceable);
   (e) Source code stored in air-gapped development environment;
   (f) Regular security audits and access logging;
   (g) "CONFIDENTIAL — TRADE SECRET" markings on all design documents;
   (h) Exit interviews with security checklist and device collection.

Zhang's Employment and Departure

9. During his tenure at TechVault, Zhang served as the lead architect of KDA-7, with access to all VaultLock source code, design documents, cryptographic specifications, and customer deployment data.

10. Zhang's employment was governed by the following agreements:
    (a) Employment Agreement dated March 1, 2022 (including confidentiality provisions)
    (b) Proprietary Information and Inventions Assignment Agreement ("PIIA") dated March 1, 2022
    (c) Non-Solicitation Agreement dated March 1, 2022 (12-month restriction)

11. On November 15, 2025, Zhang submitted his resignation, stating he intended to "take time off to explore personal projects."

12. During the period from November 15, 2025 through December 31, 2025 (Zhang's last day), TechVault's security logs reveal the following anomalous activity on Zhang's account:
    (a) November 18, 2025: Downloaded 3,847 files from VaultLock source repository (entire codebase)
    (b) November 22, 2025: Accessed customer deployment documentation for TechVault's top 15 enterprise clients
    (c) December 2, 2025: Connected personal USB storage device to work laptop (violation of policy)
    (d) December 10, 2025: Forwarded 23 emails containing VaultLock architecture diagrams to personal email (kzhang.personal@gmail.com)
    (e) December 15, 2025: Accessed KDA-7 algorithmic specification document (last accessed by Zhang on June 2025 prior to this)
    (f) December 28, 2025: Wiped browser history and cleared local cache on work laptop

13. Zhang's exit interview was conducted on December 30, 2025. Zhang certified that he had returned all company property and had not retained any TechVault confidential information. He returned his work laptop, which upon forensic examination showed evidence of mass file deletion and cache wiping.

14. On January 15, 2026, Zhang began employment at NexGen as Vice President of Engineering.

15. On February 20, 2026, NexGen announced "NexGen Fortress," a new zero-knowledge encryption platform with capabilities remarkably similar to VaultLock, including performance characteristics matching KDA-7 within 3% on industry benchmarks.

16. NexGen's public marketing materials describe Fortress as featuring a "breakthrough key derivation approach" that achieves "unprecedented speed without compromising FIPS compliance" — language that mirrors TechVault's internal descriptions of KDA-7.

17. Upon information and belief, the development timeline for NexGen Fortress is inconsistent with independent development. Industry experts estimate that developing a comparable zero-knowledge encryption platform would require 18-24 months and $8-15 million in engineering investment. NexGen announced Fortress less than 6 weeks after Zhang joined.

COUNT I — MISAPPROPRIATION OF TRADE SECRETS (Federal — DTSA)

18. Plaintiff incorporates paragraphs 1-17.
19. Defendants misappropriated TechVault's trade secrets in violation of the Defend Trade Secrets Act, 18 U.S.C. § 1836 et seq.

COUNT II — MISAPPROPRIATION OF TRADE SECRETS (California UTSA)

20. Plaintiff incorporates paragraphs 1-17.
21. Defendants misappropriated TechVault's trade secrets in violation of Cal. Civ. Code § 3426.

COUNT III — BREACH OF CONTRACT (Against Zhang)

22. Zhang breached his Employment Agreement, PIIA, and Non-Solicitation Agreement.

COUNT IV — BREACH OF DUTY OF LOYALTY (Against Zhang)

23. Zhang breached his fiduciary duty of loyalty by downloading trade secrets while still employed.

COUNT V — UNFAIR COMPETITION (Against NexGen)

24. NexGen engaged in unfair business practices in violation of Cal. Bus. & Prof. Code § 17200.

PRAYER FOR RELIEF

WHEREFORE, TechVault prays for:
1. Injunctive relief prohibiting NexGen from using, marketing, or selling NexGen Fortress;
2. Injunctive relief requiring return/destruction of all misappropriated trade secrets;
3. Compensatory damages in excess of $50,000,000;
4. Exemplary damages for willful and malicious misappropriation (DTSA § 1836(b)(3)(C));
5. Reasonable attorney's fees and costs;
6. Such other relief as this Court deems just.
