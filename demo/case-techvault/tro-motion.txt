Plaintiff TechVault Solutions, Inc. moves this Court for entry of a temporary restraining order and preliminary injunction pursuant to Fed. R. Civ. P. 65 and the Defend Trade Secrets Act, 18 U.S.C. § 1836(b)(3)(A).

I. INTRODUCTION

TechVault seeks emergency injunctive relief to prevent the continued use and dissemination of its trade secrets — the crown jewels of its business — by Defendants NexGen and Zhang. The forensic evidence is overwhelming: Zhang systematically exfiltrated TechVault's entire VaultLock codebase and proprietary KDA-7 encryption algorithm before defecting to competitor NexGen, which announced a suspiciously similar product just weeks later.

Without immediate relief, TechVault faces irreparable harm: the permanent destruction of its competitive advantage in a market where first-mover advantage and technological superiority are everything.

III. LEGAL STANDARD

A TRO and preliminary injunction are warranted where the movant establishes: (1) likelihood of success on the merits; (2) likelihood of irreparable harm; (3) the balance of equities tips in its favor; and (4) an injunction is in the public interest. Winter v. NRDC, 555 U.S. 7, 20 (2008).

Under the DTSA, the court may grant injunctive relief to "prevent any actual or threatened misappropriation" on such terms as the court deems reasonable. 18 U.S.C. § 1836(b)(3)(A).

IV. ARGUMENT

A. Likelihood of Success on the Merits

1. TechVault's VaultLock technology and KDA-7 algorithm are trade secrets.
   - $12 million and 47,000 engineering hours invested
   - Protected by access controls, NDAs, air-gapped development, and security protocols
   - Derives independent economic value from not being generally known

2. Zhang misappropriated these trade secrets.
   - Forensic evidence: 3,847 files cloned, USB transfer, 23 emails to personal account
   - Timeline: Applied to NexGen before resigning, exfiltrated data during notice period
   - False exit interview certification
   - Installation of secure deletion software to cover tracks

3. NexGen acquired and used the trade secrets.
   - Announced Fortress product 5 weeks after hiring Zhang
   - Fortress matches VaultLock capabilities within 3% on benchmarks
   - Independent development would take 18-24 months minimum
   - NexGen's marketing language mirrors TechVault internal descriptions

B. Irreparable Harm

Trade secret misappropriation, by its nature, causes irreparable harm. Once a trade secret is disclosed or used by a competitor, the harm cannot be undone by monetary damages alone. See Ruckelshaus v. Monsanto Co., 467 U.S. 986, 1012 (1984).

Specifically:
- VaultLock's competitive advantage will be permanently destroyed
- Customer confidence will be undermined
- TechVault's $12 million R&D investment will be diminished
- The market window for VaultLock's next-generation features will close

C. Balance of Equities

The balance overwhelmingly favors TechVault. NexGen can continue operating its existing product lines — only the allegedly stolen Fortress product would be enjoined. Zhang's employment at NexGen need not be terminated, but he should be prohibited from working on any encryption-related projects.

D. Public Interest

The public interest favors protecting trade secrets and enforcing contractual obligations. Allowing companies to benefit from stolen technology undermines innovation and the incentive to invest in R&D.

V. REQUESTED RELIEF

TechVault respectfully requests that this Court enter an order:

1. Prohibiting NexGen from marketing, selling, licensing, or further developing NexGen Fortress;
2. Requiring NexGen to sequester all code, documents, and materials related to Fortress pending trial;
3. Prohibiting Zhang from disclosing any TechVault trade secrets or working on encryption-related projects at NexGen;
4. Requiring Defendants to return or destroy all copies of TechVault's trade secrets;
5. Appointing a Special Master to oversee compliance;
6. Requiring expedited discovery on the scope of misappropriation.
